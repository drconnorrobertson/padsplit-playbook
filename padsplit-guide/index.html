The PadSplit Guide

What it is, how it works, and why the room-by-room rental model matters.

What is PadSplit?

PadSplit is a co-living platform that allows property owners to rent out their homes one bedroom at a time, on a weekly basis, with all utilities and internet included. The platform handles the marketing, member screening, payment collection, and a portion of the dispute resolution. Owners handle the physical property — conversion, furnishings, maintenance, and the on-the-ground community.

The members — the people who live in the homes — are typically workforce Americans earning $25,000 to $45,000 per year. They are nursing assistants, retail managers, line cooks, security guards, gig workers, and recently-housed individuals rebuilding stability. Most cannot qualify for a traditional 12-month lease at workforce price points in their city, because that price point increasingly does not exist.

PadSplit fills a real gap. The members get a private bedroom, a clean shared house, and a price they can actually afford. The owners get gross rents that are 1.5x to 2x what the same property would generate as a single-family rental.

How the model works

The mechanics are simple. You take a single-family home — typically four bedrooms, often more after conversion — and you rent each bedroom separately. Each room becomes a "member" on the platform. Members share the kitchen, the living areas, and the bathrooms.

Each bedroom is privately locked. Each member has their own payment account and their own move-in date. There is no traditional lease — members pay weekly, can leave with short notice, and can stay as long as they want. Most stay for several months. Many stay for a year or more.

The economics

A four-bedroom house that rents for $1,800 per month as a single-family rental often generates $3,200 to $4,000 per month as a PadSplit. Five-bedroom configurations push higher. The differential is the spread that pays for utilities, internet, weekly cleaning, furnishings, and the operator's time — and still leaves substantially better cash flow than the single-family alternative.

The room-by-room rental model

The thing to understand about room-by-room rental is that it's not a hack on the traditional rental model — it's a different product entirely.

A traditional rental sells a home. A PadSplit sells access to housing. The unit of inventory is the bedroom, not the unit. The pricing is weekly, not monthly. The payment is digital, not by check. The community is shared, not private. Each of those differences is meaningful, and together they produce a model with fundamentally different economics from a normal rental.

Why it works

  • It matches the way affordable housing is actually consumed. Working Americans at the $25k–$45k income level often need housing for fewer than 12 months at a time, often need to move for work, and often cannot front a security deposit and first/last month's rent.
  • It unlocks unused inventory. Single-family homes are an enormous and underutilized affordable-housing resource. Each four-bedroom house is four affordable rooms.
  • It works at scale because the operating layer is standardized. Payments, screening, and basic dispute resolution are handled by the platform. The operator focuses on the property and the community.

What it looks like from the inside

A well-run PadSplit feels like a clean, organized shared house. Each bedroom is furnished — bed, dresser, side table, lamp, smart lock. The common areas have a couch, a TV, a working kitchen with shared cookware, a washer and dryer, and reliable internet. Cleaning happens weekly. Rules are posted. Conflicts are addressed quickly.

It is not glamorous. It is not luxury. It is dignified, affordable, functional housing — and for the people living in it, that is exactly what's needed.
